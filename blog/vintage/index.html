Vintage Ephemera
09 Jun 2013

Here’s a picture of my turntable, a Technics SL-1200. It was manufactured between 1979 and 2010 and has been most commonly used for DJing. I have no idea how old mine actually is – I bought mine from eBay.

So, I bought this piece of old technology which looks really cool, but there’s really no point to it (I don’t plan on DJing with a turntable anytime soon). With record players, I gain unnoticeably higher sound quality, but I’m losing portability and convenience. I’m purposely deciding that I want my music to be stored in these large, black 12-inch records rather than the smaller, shinier CDs or the seemingly invisible space of a hard drive. So really, why? Why would I get a turntable?

Because it’s cool. There’s not too much to it. But why exactly would I find a turntable cool in this day and age?

Digital media is becoming the norm; I know more people that use Spotify and Netflix instead of going out and buying a CD or DVD (or, god forbid, renting from Blockbuster if those still exist). With this increasing digitalization, the way we interact with music, movies, and video games are becoming strictly two-dimensional. Owning a movie doesn’t mean having a plastic case with a circular disc inside it. Instead, we just click a “Purchased” button to see that we do in fact own that movie. It’s not exciting, and it doesn’t feel like there’s value to it.

For music, vinyl still exists, however, and there’s a physicality to it. Instead of getting 500x500px cover art, we get a 12x12in. piece of artwork. Instead of seeing the waveforms, you can feel the ridges1 that represent the music. And instead of the music being abstracted through a progress bar and some metadata about the mp3, you can actually see the needle moving along the ridges, progressing through the center. A turntable makes the process of playing music seem less abstract.

My Technics SL-1200 is also a remarkably well-designed turntable. Design feels more noticeable today because of computers. Many UX/UI designers are hired to essentially make the placement of pixels on a screen as pleasant as possible. There are sites focused on critiquing logos and people have the power to “undo” a logo. More people know and recognize Helvetica2 now than twenty years ago. And Apple can be very profitable by selling a very nicely designed product. I would think that the industrial design of the Technics SL-1200 is more appreciated today just because companies like Apple have made people care more about things like this.

And then there’s the fact that it’s “vintage”, whatever that means. There’s a meaning that can be gained from seeing a turntable at someone’s place, similar to the way that pianos seem to exist but not be played in a typical middle-class household. All this digital stuff means that it’s easier to create our individual online personas, but it also means that offline expression becomes less material. This might be good, this might not be.

Or maybe, you know, turntables are cool just because they are.

  2. Then again, today’s Helvetica is Gotham, and not enough people realize how it’s literally everywhere nowadays. ↩
