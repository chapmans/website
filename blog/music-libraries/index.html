Music Libraries
28 May 2013

With iTunes, Spotify, YouTube, etc., it’s easier than ever to find that one artist, one album, one song that only you connect with. You don’t have to sift into shelves of vinyl or go through a ton of CD racks at some random record store; you just need to type something into a search bar. The fact that it’s becoming so easy to be “unique” though means that our music tastes are just another bit making up our digital fingerprints.

The idea of digital fingerprints comes from how there’s enough publicly accessible data on someone’s computer that can be accessed to result in a unique configuration. EFF’s Panopticlick tests this. For example, the combination of fonts on someone’s computer, which can be grabbed through Flash, might be enough to identify them. The fact that it’s so easy to pinpoint a particular computer configuration means that it’s easy to track and specifically advertise to a particular computer – and a particular person.

The thing with music libraries is that there are so many places to share them. I really, really like Shugo Tokumaru’s Exit1; I’m one of the 27,000 people who scrobbled the album on last.fm, one of the (almost) 12,000 people who liked the Facebook page, one of many who posted a song from the album on Tumblr, and one of 52 people giving the album over four stars on Rate Your Music. It’s pretty easy to figure out who I am on all these different sites, especially with more additional information like how I also really like Amon Tobin, Shiina Ringo, and Broadcast.

This stuff – deanonymizing people based on values in a database – has been done before. So much personal data through our searches and likes are out there constantly being processed and analyzed, and it’s creepy what conclusions have been made from this data.

Music is deeply personal. It’s possible to figure out someone’s political and religious opinions from seeing what they listen to, and it probably wouldn’t be too hard to figure out someone’s general location based on when they started to listen to a particular artist2. I wonder: what other information can be inferred from, say, the fact that I listened to Exit at least 16 times during a week of November 2008? How identifiable can someone be with just a tiny piece of data?
